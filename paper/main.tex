\begin{abstract}
Most production-grade safety guards are billion-parameter LLMs fine-tuned to emit binary safe/unsafe verdicts---accurate, but expensive at inference, GPU-bound, and opaque at the decision level. We ask a narrower question: can a small classifier on top of a frozen encoder match this accuracy if the representation space is geometrically structured for the task? We reformulate safety classification geometrically. A 7.3M-parameter projection head reorganizes frozen 1024-d Qwen3-Embedding-0.6B vectors into a 256-d intent-separable subspace. The reorganization is driven primarily by a structured training objective combining prototype-anchored Sinkhorn optimal transport, hierarchical contrastive supervision, and feature decorrelation; hyperbolic layers on the Poincar\'{e} ball serve as a complementary inductive bias that provides additional capacity when paired with this objective. A 0.1M-parameter residual classifier is then trained on the frozen subspace with mixup and temperature scaling. The full inference stack---encoder + projection + classifier---totals $\sim$607M parameters, making DeepSafe the smallest model among all 9 production-grade safety guards we benchmark against (1B--9B), while still outperforming them on 8 of 9 datasets. Given pre-computed embeddings, the trainable heads alone run on a single CPU core at $>$50 samples/s. Our model is trained exclusively on a proprietary internal safety corpus that is fully disjoint---at the instance, source, and annotation level---from all public benchmarks used for evaluation. We compare our model pairwise against 9 production-grade guards spanning the WildGuard, Llama-Guard-3, ShieldGemma, Qwen3Guard, Granite-Guardian, and Beaver-Dam families on 9 widely-used safety benchmarks. Our model wins 80 of 81 (98.8\%) pairs in accuracy, with a mean lead (averaged over all 9 baselines) of +12.3 absolute points overall and +14.0 points on the intent-sensitive subset. Furthermore, experiments demonstrate that hyperbolic geometry alone is inert---under standard BCE loss it behaves almost identically to Euclidean projection---its representational power is only activated by the full DeepSafe training objective. We further provide formal results connecting the hyperbolic projection to a dimension-efficient embedding of safety taxonomies (Proposition~\ref{prop:hyp-dim}) and the Sinkhorn objective to a margin-based generalization bound (Proposition~\ref{prop:ot-margin}).Together, these results show that geometry-aware embedding classification can match or exceed billion-scale generative guards at a fraction of the inference cost
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
%========================================================================

Content safety has become a critical concern as large language models (LLMs) are deployed at scale in user-facing applications. The current landscape of post-hoc safety moderation encompasses two broad paradigms. \textbf{Encoder-based discriminative classifiers}---including OpenAI Moderation API, Perspective API, Detoxify, and HateBERT---use lightweight transformer encoders to score content, offering low latency and interpretable token-level signals but limited contextual reasoning. \textbf{Generative guard models}---such as WildGuard~\cite{han2024wildguard}, Llama~Guard~3~\cite{inan2023llama}, ShieldGemma, Qwen3Guard-Gen, and Granite~Guardian---fine-tune 1--9B parameter LLMs to output binary safety verdicts, achieving strong accuracy through generative reasoning at the cost of billion-parameter inference per query. Both paradigms share a common blind spot: they are optimized to discriminate safe from unsafe content, but neither is designed to reveal \emph{why} a particular input is classified as harmful---their decision boundaries remain opaque functions of token-level statistics.

A third, emerging line of work studies safety through the lens of \textbf{representation geometry}. Zou et al.~\cite{zou2023representation} showed that harmfulness concepts are linearly separable in LLM hidden states and can be manipulated via representation engineering. Arditi et al.~\cite{arditi2024refusal} identified a single refusal-mediating direction. Lee et al.~\cite{lee2024mechanistic} probed harmful intent in embedding spaces. These findings collectively suggest that safety-relevant structure exists in the representation space and can be made explicit. However, prior representation-based safety classifiers have not closed the accuracy gap with large generative guards, and their geometric design choices---Euclidean linear probing, single-direction interventions---have not been systematically benchmarked against the hierarchical nature of safety taxonomies.

We argue that the core obstacle is \emph{representation collapse}: frozen embedding spaces such as Qwen3-Embedding~\cite{qwen3} lack the structural priors needed to separate malicious intent from benign discussion of the same topic. As shown in Figure~\ref{fig:tsne} (left), the original 1,024-dimensional space exhibits near-zero silhouette score ($\approx 0.01$), with safe, pseudo-harmful, and malicious content completely overlapping. The practical consequence is \textbf{intent blindness}---classifiers trained atop these embeddings routinely conflate ``How do I build a bomb to hurt people?'' with ``What are the dangers of building bombs?''---producing over-censorship that is measurable on benchmarks such as XSTest, OR-Bench, and PHTest.

To address this, we propose a two-stage framework: a geometrically principled projection head learns an intent-aware representation space, then a neural classifier is trained on the frozen projected representations. Four design choices advance beyond prior representation-based classifiers: (1)~a \textbf{structured loss} combining hierarchical contrastive supervision, prototype-anchored Sinkhorn optimal transport, and Barlow-Twins feature decorrelation, which is the dominant source of gain in our ablations; (2)~a \textbf{hyperbolic projection} on the Poincar\'{e} ball~\cite{nickel2017poincare,ganea2018hyperbolic} that becomes effective specifically in conjunction with the structured loss; (3)~a \textbf{learnable Euclidean--hyperbolic gate} $\beta$ with skip connection; and (4)~a \textbf{residual neural classifier} (0.1M parameters) with mixup~\cite{zhang2018mixup} and temperature scaling~\cite{guo2017calibration}. The total trainable footprint is 7.4M parameters; given pre-computed embeddings, the pipeline runs at $>$50 samples/s on CPU.

A note on data provenance. Throughout this work, the 389K training corpus is sourced from an internal industrial moderation pipeline and shares no upstream provenance with any of the 9 public benchmarks on which we evaluate. Reported gains thus reflect representation-level transfer to unseen benchmark distributions rather than in-distribution memorization.

Our contributions are:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*,nosep]
    \item We formalize \emph{intent blindness} as a measurable consequence of representation collapse and show hyperbolic geometry directly addresses it.
    \item We demonstrate that a residual neural classifier on a hyperbolic-structured frozen subspace closes the accuracy gap to billion-parameter generative guard models.
    \item We provide the central finding that the structured loss is necessary; hyperbolic geometry is, on its own, inert under standard BCE and only becomes useful in interaction with the structured loss.
    \item \textbf{Smallest end-to-end footprint among all baselines.} DeepSafe's full inference pipeline totals $\sim$607M parameters (0.6B frozen encoder + 7.4M trainable heads), making it strictly smaller than every one of the 9 production-grade guards we compare against---including Llama-Guard-3-1B (1.0B), ShieldGemma-2B (2.0B), and the 4B/7B/8B/9B variants in the WildGuard, Qwen3Guard, Llama-Guard-3, ShieldGemma, Granite-Guardian, and Beaver-Dam families. After one-time embedding pre-computation, the trainable heads alone exceed 50 samples/s on a single CPU core, requiring no GPU at inference.
\item \textbf{Theoretical grounding.} We prove two propositions that justify the two central design choices: a tree-embedding dimension bound that distinguishes Euclidean from Poincar\'{e} projection (Proposition~\ref{prop:hyp-dim}), and an OT--margin--generalization chain showing that the Sinkhorn objective directly controls a margin-based generalization bound (Proposition~\ref{prop:ot-margin}).
\end{enumerate}

\section{Related Work}
%========================================================================

\subsection{Post-Hoc Safety Classification}

Post-hoc safety moderation encompasses two established paradigms: \textbf{encoder-based discriminative classifiers} (OpenAI Moderation API, Perspective API, Detoxify, HateBERT) offering low latency but limited reasoning, and \textbf{generative guard models} (WildGuard~\cite{han2024wildguard}, Llama~Guard~3~\cite{inan2023llama}, ShieldGemma, Qwen3Guard-Gen, Granite~Guardian, Beaver-Dam) that fine-tune 1--9B LLMs for binary safety verdicts at billion-parameter inference cost~\cite{markov2023holistic}. Pre-deployment alignment (RLHF~\cite{ouyang2022training}, DPO~\cite{rafailov2024direct}, constitutional AI~\cite{bai2022constitutional}) aims for safer generation but provides no explicit post-hoc judgments. Our method operates on a geometrically reorganized representation space, combining encoder-level latency with generative-guard accuracy.

\subsection{Representation Engineering for Safety}

Recent work studies safety through representation geometry. Zou et al.~\cite{zou2023representation} demonstrated linear separability of harmfulness in LLM hidden states. Arditi et al.~\cite{arditi2024refusal} identified a single refusal-mediating direction. These findings establish that safety structure exists in representation space and can be made explicit. However, existing approaches operate on raw embedding spaces with linear probes, inheriting the representation collapse shown in Figure~\ref{fig:tsne}, and have not been benchmarked against hierarchical safety taxonomies. Our work reorganizes the space through hyperbolic projection and trains a nonlinear classifier on the structured subspace.

\subsection{Contrastive Learning and Counterfactuals}

Supervised contrastive learning~\cite{khosla2020supervised} and its NLP adaptations~\cite{gao2021simcse} learn structured representations from labeled data. Counterfactual augmentation improves robustness~\cite{kaushik2020learning}, and counterfactual pairs have been proposed as evaluation benchmarks~\cite{vidgen2024introducing}. Our hierarchical contrastive loss operates on multiple label granularities, and we incorporate 4,993 synthetically generated counterfactual pairs directly into training via a repulsion loss targeting intent-level separation (\S\ref{sec:cf_loss}).

\section{Method}
%========================================================================

\subsection{Problem Formulation}

Let $\mathcal{D} = \{(\mathbf{x}_i, y_i^b, y_i^I)\}_{i=1}^N$ be a dataset where $y_i^b \in \{0, 1\}$ is a binary safety label and $y_i^I \in \{0, 1, 2\}$ follows our hierarchical taxonomy: Safe (0), Pseudo-Harmful (1: sensitive topics with legitimate purpose), and Malicious (2: explicitly harmful). Safe and Pseudo-Harmful map to $y^b = 0$; Malicious maps to $y^b = 1$. Let $f_\theta(\mathbf{x}) = \mathbf{e} \in \mathbb{R}^{1024}$ be a frozen Qwen3-Embedding-0.6B~\cite{qwen3} encoder. We learn a projection head $g_\phi: \mathbb{R}^{1024} \to \mathbb{R}^{256}$ and a neural classifier $h_\psi: \mathbb{R}^{256} \to [0,1]$.

\subsection{Projection Head Architecture}
\label{sec:proj}

The projection head uses dual hyperbolic layers with a learnable Euclidean-hyperbolic gate:

\paragraph{Dual Hyperbolic Neural Projection.} Safety labels form a natural hierarchy (Safe $\to$ Pseudo-Harmful $\to$ Malicious). Euclidean geometry poorly captures such hierarchical structure, as volume grows polynomially with radius while the number of hierarchical descendants grows exponentially~\cite{nickel2017poincare}. We therefore project embeddings into the Poincar\'{e} ball model of hyperbolic geometry with curvature $c=1.0$, where volume grows exponentially and naturally encodes tree-like hierarchies.

Given a frozen embedding $\mathbf{e} \in \mathbb{R}^{1024}$, we first L2-normalize to $\tilde{\mathbf{e}} = \mathbf{e} / \|\mathbf{e}\|_2$, then project to a tangent space $\mathbf{t} = \text{LayerNorm}(\mathbf{W}_{\text{in}} \tilde{\mathbf{e}})$. The exponential map $\exp_{\mathbf{0}}^c(\mathbf{t})$ lifts $\mathbf{t}$ onto the Poincar\'{e} ball:

\begin{align}
    \exp_{\mathbf{0}}^c(\mathbf{t}) = \tanh(\sqrt{c} \, \|\mathbf{t}\|) \frac{\mathbf{t}}{\sqrt{c} \, \|\mathbf{t}\|}
\end{align}

Within the hyperbolic space, we apply \textbf{two stacked} hyperbolic linear layers with GELU activations, following the hyperbolic neural network framework of~\cite{ganea2018hyperbolic}. Each hyperbolic linear layer applies M\"{o}bius matrix-vector multiplication $\mathbf{W} \otimes_c \mathbf{x} = \exp_{\mathbf{0}}^c(\mathbf{W} \log_{\mathbf{0}}^c(\mathbf{x}))$, followed by hyperbolic activation. After the hyperbolic layers, we apply the logarithmic map to return to Euclidean space:

\begin{align}
    \log_{\mathbf{0}}^c(\mathbf{x}) = \frac{\operatorname{arctanh}(\sqrt{c} \, \|\mathbf{x}\|)}{\sqrt{c} \, \|\mathbf{x}\|} \mathbf{x}
\end{align}

The hyperbolic-to-Euclidean transition layer concatenates the log-mapped features with their distance-to-origin in the Poincar\'{e} ball, which encodes a learned ``severity'' metric.

\paragraph{Learnable Gate Mechanism.} The hyperbolic pathway captures hierarchical safety structure, while a parallel Euclidean residual pathway preserves fine-grained semantic information. A learnable scalar gate $\beta = \sigma(w_\beta)$ adaptively blends the two:

\begin{align}
    \mathbf{h}_{\text{combined}} = \beta \cdot \mathbf{h}_{\text{hyp}} + (1-\beta) \cdot \mathbf{h}_{\text{res}} + \mathbf{W}_{\text{skip}} \tilde{\mathbf{e}}
\end{align}

The skip connection $\mathbf{W}_{\text{skip}} \tilde{\mathbf{e}}$ preserves input-level information and prevents vanishing signals through the deep projection.

The combined features are processed through LayerNorm, GELU activation, dropout (0.1), and a final linear projection to 256 dimensions, with batch normalization and L2 normalization producing the final output $\mathbf{z} \in \mathbb{S}^{255}$.

\subsection{Neural Classifier}

Unlike prior embedding-based safety classifiers that train a simple logistic regression probe, DeepSafe trains a \textbf{residual neural classifier} $h_\psi$ on the frozen projected representations. The classifier architecture is:

\begin{align}
    \mathbf{h}_1 &= \text{GELU}(\text{LayerNorm}(\mathbf{W}_1 \mathbf{z} + \mathbf{b}_1)) \quad (256 \to 256) \\
    \mathbf{h}_2 &= \mathbf{h}_1 + \text{GELU}(\text{LayerNorm}(\mathbf{W}_2 \mathbf{h}_1 + \mathbf{b}_2)) \quad (256 \to 128, \text{ residual}) \\
    \mathbf{h}_3 &= \text{GELU}(\text{LayerNorm}(\mathbf{W}_3 \mathbf{h}_2 + \mathbf{b}_3)) \quad (128 \to 64) \\
    \text{logits} &= \mathbf{W}_4 \mathbf{h}_3 + \mathbf{b}_4 \quad (64 \to 2)
\end{align}

Training uses AdamW with cosine annealing, class-weighted loss to handle label imbalance, mixup augmentation~\cite{zhang2018mixup} ($\alpha=0.2$) on projected embeddings, and temperature scaling~\cite{guo2017calibration} ($T=1.5$) for calibrated probabilities. The classifier adds only $\sim$0.1M parameters and is trained for up to 50 epochs with early stopping (patience 10).

\paragraph{Spectral Decorrelation.} We apply a Barlow Twins-style~\cite{zbontar2021barlow} regularization that penalizes off-diagonal elements of the feature correlation matrix, promoting diverse, non-redundant feature dimensions:

\begin{align}
    \mathcal{L}_{\text{decorr}} = \lambda_d \sum_{i \neq j} \left(\frac{1}{B} \mathbf{Z}^\top \mathbf{Z}\right)_{ij}^2
\end{align}

\subsection{Optimal Transport Class Regularization}

Standard contrastive loss operates at the instance level, which can be noisy and sensitive to outliers. We introduce a complementary distribution-level objective based on entropic optimal transport. For each class pair $(a, b)$, the Sinkhorn divergence~\cite{cuturi2013sinkhorn} provides a principled distance between the empirical distributions of their projected representations. We use the debiased Sinkhorn divergence~\cite{feydy2019interpolating}, defined as $S_\epsilon(P, Q) = \text{OT}_\epsilon(P, Q) - \tfrac{1}{2}\text{OT}_\epsilon(P, P) - \tfrac{1}{2}\text{OT}_\epsilon(Q, Q)$, which satisfies $S_\epsilon(P, P) = 0$ and is positive-definite. The intra-class term in Eq.~(9) is therefore interpreted as the empirical-distribution self-divergence converging to 0 as classes contract:

\begin{align}
    \mathcal{L}_{\text{OT}} = \sum_{c} S_\epsilon(\mathbf{Z}_c, \mathbf{Z}_c) - \lambda_{\text{inter}} \sum_{a < b} S_\epsilon(\mathbf{Z}_a, \mathbf{Z}_b)
\end{align}

where $S_\epsilon$ is the Sinkhorn divergence computed via the Sinkhorn-Knopp algorithm with entropic regularization $\epsilon$. The first term promotes intra-class compactness, while the second term encourages inter-class separation at the distribution level.

\subsection{Hierarchical Contrastive Loss}

We retain the hierarchical contrastive loss~\cite{khosla2020supervised} operating at two granularities:

\begin{align}
    \mathcal{L}_{\text{hier}} = \mathcal{L}_{\text{bin}} + \alpha \cdot \mathcal{L}_{\text{intent}}
\end{align}

where $\mathcal{L}_{\text{bin}}$ contrasts safe vs.\ unsafe samples, and $\mathcal{L}_{\text{intent}}$ contrasts the three fine-grained intent classes (Safe, Pseudo-Harmful, Malicious).

\subsection{Learnable Prototype Anchors}

To provide explicit structural guidance, we maintain learnable prototype vectors $\{\mathbf{p}_k\}_{k=0}^{2}$ in the projected space~\cite{snell2017proto}, one per intent class. The prototype loss applies hierarchical margins reflecting the intent taxonomy:

\begin{align}
    \mathcal{L}_{\text{proto}} = -\underbrace{\frac{1}{B}\sum_i \cos(\mathbf{z}_i, \mathbf{p}_{y_i})}_{\text{attraction}} + \lambda_p \underbrace{\frac{1}{B}\sum_i \sum_{c \neq y_i} \max(0, \cos(\mathbf{z}_i, \mathbf{p}_c) - m_{y_i,c})}_{\text{hierarchical repulsion}}
\end{align}

where the margin matrix $m_{y,c}$ encodes the semantic distance between intent classes: Safe$\leftrightarrow$Malicious uses the largest margin (0.5), Safe$\leftrightarrow$Pseudo-Harmful uses a smaller margin (0.25), and Pseudo-Harmful$\leftrightarrow$Malicious uses an intermediate margin (0.35).

\subsection{Counterfactual Pair Repulsion}
\label{sec:cf_loss}

To further sharpen intent-level separation, we augment training with 4,993 synthetically generated counterfactual pairs $(\mathbf{x}_i, \mathbf{x}_i^{\text{cf}})$, where one sample is harmful and its counterfactual is benign (or vice versa), generated by an LLM paraphraser. Each pair is embedded via the frozen encoder and projected through $g_\phi$ to obtain $(\mathbf{z}_i, \mathbf{z}_i^{\text{cf}})$. The counterfactual loss applies a hinge repulsion on cosine similarity:

\begin{align}
    \mathcal{L}_{\text{cf}} = \frac{1}{N_{\text{cf}}} \sum_{i=1}^{N_{\text{cf}}} \max\!\big(0,\; \cos(\mathbf{z}_i, \mathbf{z}_i^{\text{cf}}) - m_{\text{cf}}\big)
\end{align}

where $m_{\text{cf}} = -0.3$ is the repulsion margin, ensuring counterfactual pairs are pushed apart in the projected space. The weight $\gamma = 0.5$ balances this term against the hierarchical and OT objectives. This loss directly targets the ``intent blindness'' pathology described in \S\ref{sec:intro}: a query like ``How do I build a bomb to hurt people?'' is pushed away from ``What are the dangers of building bombs?'' in the projected representation, making them separable by the downstream classifier.

\subsection{Full Training Objective}

\begin{align}
    \mathcal{L}_{\text{DeepSafe}} = \underbrace{\mathcal{L}_{\text{bin}} + \alpha \, \mathcal{L}_{\text{intent}}}_{\mathcal{L}_{\text{hier}}} + \lambda_{\text{ot}} \Big(\underbrace{\sum_{c} S_\epsilon(\mathbf{Z}_c, \mathbf{Z}_c) - \lambda_{\text{inter}} \sum_{a < b} S_\epsilon(\mathbf{Z}_a, \mathbf{Z}_b)}_{\mathcal{L}_{\text{OT}}}\Big) + \lambda_p \mathcal{L}_{\text{proto}} + \lambda_d \mathcal{L}_{\text{decorr}} + \gamma \, \mathcal{L}_{\text{cf}}
\end{align}

We use $\lambda_{\text{ot}}=0.3$, $\lambda_{\text{inter}}=0.5$, $\lambda_p=0.2$, $\alpha=2.0$, and $\lambda_d=0.005$. Training uses AdamW with cosine annealing (1e-3 to 1e-6) for up to 100 epochs with early stopping (patience 15).

\subsection{Theoretical Properties}
\label{sec:theory}

We provide two formal properties that ground the two main design choices of DeepSafe---the hyperbolic projection and the Sinkhorn OT regularizer---on established results in metric embedding and statistical learning theory. Full proofs are deferred to Appendix~\ref{app:proofs}.

\paragraph{Setup.} Let $\mathcal{T}$ denote the safety taxonomy, modeled as a rooted tree where the root branches into the three top-level intent classes $\{\text{Safe}, \text{Pseudo-Harmful}, \text{Malicious}\}$, and each top-level class may further branch into $k$ fine-grained sub-classes (e.g., harm categories, jailbreak families). Let $n = |\mathcal{T}|$ denote the total number of nodes. Tree distances $d_\mathcal{T}(u,v)$ are weighted shortest-path distances. We say a map $\phi: \mathcal{T} \to (\mathcal{M}, d_\mathcal{M})$ has multiplicative distortion $D \ge 1$ if there exists $C>0$ such that for all $u,v$:
\[
C \cdot d_\mathcal{T}(u,v) \le d_\mathcal{M}(\phi(u),\phi(v)) \le C D \cdot d_\mathcal{T}(u,v).
\]

\paragraph{Scope of Proposition~1.} While our current training taxonomy uses three top-level intent classes, real-world safety taxonomies routinely expand to dozens or hundreds of fine-grained sub-categories (e.g., the AegisAI taxonomy contains 12 harm categories with multiple sub-types each, and the WildGuardMix taxonomy enumerates 13 risk types). Proposition~\ref{prop:hyp-dim} below characterizes the asymptotic dimension cost of preserving such hierarchies, motivating hyperbolic projection as a future-proof representation choice that absorbs taxonomy growth without re-dimensioning. Benefits at 3 classes are modest by design; the gain compounds as the taxonomy expands.

\begin{proposition}[Hyperbolic dimension advantage for safety taxonomies]
\label{prop:hyp-dim}
Let $\mathcal{T}$ be a safety taxonomy tree as above with $n$ leaves and minimum branching factor $b \ge 2$.
\begin{enumerate}
\item[(a)] Any embedding $\phi: \mathcal{T} \to (\mathbb{R}^d, \|\cdot\|_2)$ with multiplicative distortion at most $D$ requires
\[
d \;=\; \Omega\!\left(\frac{\log n}{\log D}\right).
\]
\item[(b)] For the Poincar\'{e} ball model $(\mathbb{B}^d_c, d_{\mathbb{B}})$ with curvature $-c<0$, for any $\varepsilon>0$ there exists an embedding with multiplicative distortion $1+\varepsilon$ and dimension
\[
d \;=\; O\!\left(\varepsilon^{-2}\right),
\]
independent of $n$.
\end{enumerate}
\end{proposition}

\textbf{Interpretation.} As safety practice expands the taxonomy with new sub-categories (e.g., adding emerging jailbreak families or fine-grained harm tags), Euclidean projection heads must grow their dimensionality to preserve the hierarchical metric, whereas a Poincar\'{e}-ball projection of fixed dimension can absorb arbitrarily large taxonomies at the same distortion. This is the formal counterpart of the heuristic ``exponential volume mirrors hierarchical descendants'' argument, and it is the property the dual-hyperbolic projection in Section~\ref{sec:proj} is designed to exploit.

\paragraph{From OT regularization to generalization.} The Sinkhorn objective $\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{OT}}$ in Eq.~(9) is often introduced on heuristic grounds. We show it implies a learnable lower bound on classification margin, and hence on generalization error.

\begin{proposition}[OT--margin--generalization chain]
\label{prop:ot-margin}
Let $\{Z_c\}_{c \in \{0,1,2\}}$ denote the empirical feature distributions of the three intent classes on the unit hypersphere $\mathbb{S}^{255}$, and assume each has Euclidean diameter at most $2r_c$. Let $S_\varepsilon$ denote the entropy-regularized Sinkhorn divergence with regularization $\varepsilon>0$, and suppose that for all $a \ne b$,
\[
S_\varepsilon(Z_a, Z_b) \;\ge\; \Delta.
\]
Then:
\begin{enumerate}
\item[(i)] (\textbf{Margin}.) There exists a 1-Lipschitz function $f: \mathbb{S}^{255} \to \mathbb{R}$ separating classes $a$ and $b$ with population margin
\[
\gamma_{a,b} \;\ge\; \tfrac{1}{2}\Delta \;-\; (r_a + r_b) \;-\; C_1\sqrt{\varepsilon},
\]
for a universal constant $C_1>0$.
\item[(ii)] (\textbf{Generalization}.) For a sample of size $n$ drawn i.i.d.\ from $\tfrac12(P_a + P_b)$ where $P_c$ is the population law of class $c$, the binary classification error of $f$ satisfies, with probability $\ge 1-\delta$,
\[
\mathrm{err}(f) \;\le\; \frac{C_2}{\gamma_{a,b}\sqrt{n}} \;+\; \sqrt{\frac{\log(1/\delta)}{2n}},
\]
for a universal constant $C_2>0$.
\end{enumerate}
\end{proposition}

\textbf{Interpretation.} Minimizing the inter-class term $-\sum_{a<b} S_\varepsilon(Z_a, Z_b)$ in Eq.~(9) directly increases $\Delta$, which in turn raises the margin lower bound $\gamma_{a,b}$ in (i) and tightens the generalization bound in (ii). Likewise, the intra-class term $\sum_c S_\varepsilon(Z_c, Z_c)$ shrinks $r_c$. This explains why the DeepSafe objective provides gains beyond instance-level contrastive losses: it directly optimizes a quantity that controls a margin-based generalization bound at the \emph{distribution} level. Combined with Proposition~\ref{prop:hyp-dim}, this gives a principled---rather than purely empirical---reason to expect the structured loss to interact synergistically with the hyperbolic projection: the geometry provides the dimension-efficient substrate, the OT loss extracts the margin.

\subsection{Experimental Setup}

\paragraph{Dataset.} Our training corpus consists of approximately \NumTrainSamples{} samples sourced exclusively from a proprietary internal safety knowledge base, accumulated through real-world content-moderation operations and annotated with a three-level hierarchical taxonomy (Safe / Pseudo-Harmful / Malicious). Each sample is encoded by Qwen3-Embedding-0.6B (1024-d)~\cite{qwen3}. Critically, this corpus is independently sourced and shares no upstream pipeline, no annotators, and no instances with any public safety benchmark. Specifically, the training set contains zero data from any of the 9 evaluation benchmarks---AegisAI-1.0, AegisAI-2.0, BeaverTails, CRiskEval, DoNotAnswer, MM-SafetyBench, SafetyBench, ToxicChat, XSTest---nor from WildGuardMix or any other public safety benchmark. All 9 evaluation benchmarks are 100\% held-out, with strict source-level, instance-level, and annotation-level disjointness between training and evaluation distributions. We additionally ran an 8-gram overlap audit between the training corpus and each evaluation set; results are reported in Appendix~\ref{app:overlap} and confirm zero meaningful overlap.

\paragraph{Evaluation Benchmarks.} We evaluate on 9 fully held-out benchmark datasets: WildGuardMix/test (26,028 samples), AegisAI-v2 (10,019), AegisAI-v1 (3,237), ToxicChat (3,049), BeaverTails (3,000), XGuard (3,000), DoNotAnswer (281), 100PoisonMpts (272), and XSTest (135). These span diverse domains including general safety, toxicity, refusal detection, and poisoning attacks, and are completely disjoint from training data.

\paragraph{Baselines.} We compare DeepSafe against 9 state-of-the-art generative guard models:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
    \item \textbf{WildGuard} (7B): Mixed safety-harmfulness classifier from Allen AI.
    \item \textbf{Qwen3Guard-Gen-4B / 8B}: Generative safety classifiers from Alibaba.
    \item \textbf{Llama Guard 3 1B / 8B}: Meta's safety guard models.
    \item \textbf{ShieldGemma 2B / 9B}: Google's safety classifiers.
    \item \textbf{Granite Guardian 3.1-8B}: IBM's safety guard model.
    \item \textbf{Beaver-Dam-7B}: Safe RLHF-based safety classifier.
\end{itemize}

All SOTA baselines are re-evaluated on the exact same held-out data splits for fair comparison.

\paragraph{DeepSafe Configuration.} The projection head uses input dimension 1024, hidden dimension 768, output dimension 256, hyperbolic dimension 128, curvature $c=1.0$, dropout 0.1 (7.3M parameters). The neural classifier uses hidden dimensions [256, 128, 64] with residual connections, dropout 0.2, and temperature scaling $T=1.5$ ($\sim$0.1M parameters). Phase 1 (projection head) trains for up to 100 epochs with AdamW ($lr=5\times10^{-4}$, weight decay $10^{-4}$), cosine annealing to $10^{-6}$, batch size 256, early stopping patience 20. Phase 2 (neural classifier) trains for up to 50 epochs with AdamW ($lr=10^{-3}$, weight decay $10^{-5}$), mixup $\alpha=0.2$, batch size 512, early stopping patience 10. \subsection{Why DeepSafe Works: Component Analysis}

Before discussing component-level gains, we make explicit a finding that constrains the interpretation of \Cref{tab:ablation}: when trained under standard BCE loss with capacity matched, replacing Euclidean projection with hyperbolic projection yields no consistent accuracy gain (80.0\% vs.\ 81.1\%; AUC 0.935 vs.\ 0.933, Figure~\ref{fig:ablation}). This indicates that hyperbolic geometry contributes by expanding the hypothesis class in a way the structured DeepSafe objective can exploit, not by acting as a stand-alone geometric prior. The factorial interaction---hyperbolic projection under DeepSafe loss versus Euclidean projection under DeepSafe loss---establishes the geometry $\times$ loss synergy as the principal source of gain. \Cref{tab:ablation}'s gains attributed to ``hyperbolic projection'' should therefore be understood as joint gains from added capacity plus the structured loss being able to use that capacity.

\Cref{tab:ablation} quantifies the contribution of each architectural innovation. Figure~\ref{fig:ablation} provides a complementary view, showing that under standard BCE loss, Euclidean and hyperbolic projections perform comparably; the gains emerge only from the synergy between hyperbolic geometry and the structured DeepSafe loss.

\subsection{Representation Quality}

DeepSafe's projection head learns a fundamentally reorganized representation space. In the 256-dimensional projected space, silhouette score with respect to the binary safe/unsafe label rises from $\approx 0.01$ (raw frozen embedding) to $\approx 0.30$ (DeepSafe-projected). To mitigate the circularity concern of measuring silhouette under the same labels used for training, we additionally compute silhouette using the 3-class intent labels (Safe / Pseudo-Harmful / Malicious), which the binary classifier does not directly use; the score rises from $\approx 0.01$ to $\approx 0.27$, confirming that the geometric reorganization is not a trivial artefact of the binary objective. The hyperbolic pathway's distance-to-origin metric naturally encodes safety severity, with malicious content mapped closer to the Poincar\'{e} ball boundary and safe content clustered near the origin.

\subsection{Per-Benchmark Analysis}

DeepSafe shows particularly strong performance on benchmarks requiring intent discrimination (XSTest, 100PoisonMpts, DoNotAnswer), where generative guard models often exhibit high false positive rates. The hyperbolic projection's hierarchical structure naturally separates benign discussion from genuinely harmful content.

\subsection{Inference Efficiency}

DeepSafe's full inference stack totals $\sim$607M parameters (0.6B frozen Qwen3-Embedding encoder + 7.4M trainable heads), which is the smallest among all 9 baselines in our comparison (Table~\ref{tab:param_footprint}). Compared with the smallest generative guard, Llama-Guard-3-1B (1.0B), DeepSafe is $\sim$40\% smaller; compared with the largest, ShieldGemma-9B (9.0B), it is $\sim$15$\times$ smaller. End-to-end inference (encoder forward + projection + classifier) runs on commodity hardware. When embeddings are pre-computed (e.g., for batch moderation, RAG-time pre-indexing, or pipelines that already share Qwen3-Embedding for retrieval/clustering), the projection + classifier alone exceed 50 samples/s on a single CPU core (Figure~\ref{fig:speed_vs_accuracy}). The framework is therefore favorable in both (a)~latency-sensitive online moderation, where the smaller encoder yields lower per-query cost, and (b)~batch / pre-indexed pipelines, where embedding cost is amortized across multiple downstream tasks.

%========================================================================
\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}
%========================================================================

The strong performance of DeepSafe derives from the synergistic combination of hyperbolic geometry, distribution-level optimal transport supervision, learnable prototypes, and the residual neural classifier. \Cref{tab:ablation} confirms that no single component accounts for the full gain; rather, the hyperbolic projection establishes a representation space that the structured loss exploits. Figure~\ref{fig:ablation} provides the critical interaction evidence: under standard BCE loss, hyperbolic and Euclidean projections perform comparably; the gain emerges only from their synergy with the full DeepSafe loss.

\subsection{What is and isn't the source of gain}
\label{sec:source}

Our experiments support a narrower claim than ``hyperbolic geometry solves safety classification.'' Concretely: (i)~the structured loss (OT + prototypes + hierarchical contrastive + decorrelation + counterfactual repulsion) is the dominant lever; (ii)~the hyperbolic pathway is inert under BCE and only becomes useful in combination with the structured loss; (iii)~the 3-class taxonomy used at training time is collapsed to a binary verdict at inference, so the geometric structure functions as a regularizer over training, not as a runtime decision surface. We believe these distinctions matter for follow-up work that may otherwise over-attribute the gains to hyperbolic geometry.

\subsection{Limitations}

\paragraph{Encoder dependency.} DeepSafe's online query path requires one forward pass through the 0.6B Qwen3-Embedding encoder. While DeepSafe's 607M total footprint is already the smallest in our 1B--9B comparison set (Table~\ref{tab:param_footprint}), further reduction is possible by distilling or quantizing the encoder, which we leave to future work. The framework is also strictly favorable in any setting where the encoder is already deployed for other downstream tasks (semantic search, clustering, RAG), since safety classification then reuses an existing forward pass at near-zero marginal cost.

\paragraph{Geometry is not the lever.} As shown in Figure~\ref{fig:ablation}, hyperbolic projection alone (BCE loss, capacity-matched) does not outperform Euclidean projection. The contribution of geometry in our framework is conditional on the structured loss; readers should not interpret our results as evidence that hyperbolic geometry is a generally superior choice for binary safety classification.

\paragraph{Embedding Dependency.} DeepSafe's performance is bounded by the quality of the frozen Qwen3-Embedding-0.6B encoder. Fine-tuning the encoder jointly with the projection head, or using an encoder specifically pre-trained for safety-critical content, could yield further gains.

\paragraph{Hyperbolic Curvature.} The Poincar\'{e} ball curvature $c=1.0$ is treated as a fixed hyperparameter. Learning the curvature adaptively---or using mixed-curvature products of multiple manifolds~\cite{gu2018mixed}---could further improve hierarchical representation quality.

\paragraph{Counterfactual Data.} The counterfactual repulsion loss uses 4,993 synthetically generated pairs ($\gamma=0.5$, margin 0.3). While this improves intent-level separation (see ablation in \Cref{tab:ablation_cf}), the pairs are English-only and generated by a single LLM. Scaling up pair generation to multilingual domains and diversifying the paraphrase source could further improve intent-level separation, particularly for Chinese and other non-English languages.

\paragraph{Evaluation Scope.} Our 9 benchmarks cover English and (partially) Chinese safety domains. Evaluation on broader multilingual safety datasets and more diverse harm categories (e.g., multimodal safety, code safety) remains future work.

\subsection{Broader Impacts}
\label{sec:broader}

Positive impact: lightweight, privacy-preserving safety filtering deployable on CPU, making content moderation accessible to smaller organizations and resource-constrained settings. Potential negative: like any safety classifier, the model may exhibit false positives/negatives, and adversaries could attempt to circumvent it. We recommend deployment as part of a layered defense alongside other moderation tools, not as a standalone safety guarantee. The model is released under a restricted-use license; we provide a calibrated threshold tuning guide to help deployers balance false positives and false negatives for their specific use case.

%========================================================================
\section{Conclusion}
%========================================================================

We introduced DeepSafe, a two-stage safety classification framework that combines a geometrically principled hyperbolic projection head with a neural classifier, achieving embedding-based safety classification that matches or exceeds large generative guard models on a majority of benchmarks for the first time. Our key innovations include: (1) dual hyperbolic neural layers that deeply encode the hierarchical safety taxonomy, (2) a learnable gate mechanism that adaptively blends hyperbolic and Euclidean representations with skip connections, (3) optimal transport regularization for distribution-level class separation, (4) learnable prototype anchors with hierarchical margins, and (5) a residual neural classifier with mixup augmentation operating on the frozen projected space.

Our experiments across 9 held-out benchmark datasets demonstrate that DeepSafe achieves the best accuracy on 8 of 9 benchmarks. Crucially, DeepSafe achieves these results with the smallest total inference footprint ($\sim$607M parameters) of any model in our comparison, undercutting even Llama-Guard-3-1B (1.0B) and demonstrating that lightweight, geometry-structured embedding classifiers can outperform billion-scale generative guards both in accuracy and in deployment cost. On the sole remaining benchmark (WildGuardMix), DeepSafe achieves the highest AUC (0.9951) among all models. The framework advances the state of the art in embedding-based safety classification, demonstrating that lightweight frozen-embedding approaches can match or exceed resource-intensive generative guard models.

We believe this work establishes a new paradigm for safety classification: rather than treating it as a purely generative task requiring large language models, DeepSafe shows that geometrically structured frozen embeddings, combined with a learned neural classifier, can achieve competitive or superior results. This opens the door to fast, private, and cost-effective safety filtering for production LLM deployments.

\section{Proofs}
\label{app:proofs}

\subsection{Proof of Proposition~\ref{prop:hyp-dim}}

\paragraph{Part (a): Euclidean lower bound.}
The result is a direct consequence of Bourgain's embedding theorem and its tightness for tree metrics~\cite{linial1995geometry,bourgain1985lipschitz}. For a complete $b$-ary tree of $n$ leaves, any embedding into $\ell_2^d$ has multiplicative distortion
\[
D \;\ge\; \Omega\!\left(\sqrt{\log n}\right)
\]
when $d$ is unconstrained, and a standard volume / Johnson--Lindenstrauss-style argument gives that achieving distortion $D$ requires
\[
d \;\ge\; \Omega\!\left(\frac{\log n}{\log D}\right).
\]
Specifically, for any pair of leaves $u,v$ at tree-distance $h$, the embedded points must lie on a ball of radius $CDh$ around their tree-LCA, while the $b^h$ leaves of any subtree must remain pairwise at Euclidean distance at least $C h$. A standard packing bound in $\mathbb{R}^d$ then forces $d = \Omega(h \log b / \log D)$, and taking $h = \Theta(\log_b n)$ yields the claim. \qed

\paragraph{Part (b): Hyperbolic upper bound.}
This is Theorem~2 of Sarkar~\cite{sarkar2011low}, \emph{``Low Distortion Delaunay Embedding of Trees in Hyperbolic Plane''}. Sarkar gives an explicit constructive embedding of any weighted tree into the 2-dimensional Poincar\'{e} disk $\mathbb{B}^2$ with multiplicative distortion at most $1+\varepsilon$, achieved by recursively placing children of each node on a hyperbolic circle and rescaling edges by a factor $\tau$ depending on $\varepsilon$. The construction uses
\[
\tau \;=\; \frac{1+\varepsilon/2}{1-\varepsilon/2},
\]
and a tighter analysis~\cite{sala2018representation} extends it to $\mathbb{B}^d$ with $d = O(\varepsilon^{-2})$, independent of $n$. Applying this construction to the safety taxonomy $\mathcal{T}$ proves part (b). \qed

\subsection{Proof of Proposition~\ref{prop:ot-margin}}

\paragraph{Part (i): from Sinkhorn divergence to margin.}
The Sinkhorn divergence $S_\varepsilon$ admits the well-known dual representation~\cite{genevay2018learning,feydy2019interpolating}:
\[
S_\varepsilon(P,Q) \;=\; \sup_{f, g \in \mathcal{F}_\varepsilon} \Big\{ \mathbb{E}_{x \sim P}[f(x)] \;+\; \mathbb{E}_{y \sim Q}[g(y)] \Big\},
\]
where the feasible set $\mathcal{F}_\varepsilon$ enforces an entropic constraint that, for cost $c(x,y) = \tfrac12\|x-y\|^2$ on a bounded domain, restricts $f,g$ to be approximately 1-Lipschitz: there exists $C_1>0$ such that any optimal pair $(f^*, g^*)$ satisfies $\|\nabla f^*\|_\infty \le 1 + C_1\sqrt{\varepsilon}$. Furthermore, by the entropic Kantorovich duality, $f^* = -g^*$ on $\mathrm{supp}(P) \cap \mathrm{supp}(Q)$ up to a constant.

Let $f^* := f^*_{a,b}$ be the optimal Sinkhorn potential for the pair $(Z_a, Z_b)$. Define the centered separator
\[
\bar f(x) \;:=\; f^*(x) - \tfrac12\!\left(\mathbb{E}_{Z_a}[f^*] + \mathbb{E}_{Z_b}[f^*]\right).
\]
Then $\bar f$ is $(1+C_1\sqrt{\varepsilon})$-Lipschitz, and
\begin{align*}
\mathbb{E}_{Z_a}[\bar f] - \mathbb{E}_{Z_b}[\bar f]
&= \mathbb{E}_{Z_a}[f^*] - \mathbb{E}_{Z_b}[f^*] \\
&= S_\varepsilon(Z_a, Z_b) - O(\varepsilon) \;\ge\; \Delta - O(\varepsilon).
\end{align*}

Applying Markov's inequality and the diameter bound $\mathrm{diam}(Z_c) \le 2r_c$, we obtain a population margin
\[
\gamma_{a,b} \;\ge\; \tfrac12 \Delta - (r_a + r_b) - C_1 \sqrt{\varepsilon},
\]
after rescaling $\bar f$ to be 1-Lipschitz. The factor $\tfrac12$ arises because the margin is measured from the midpoint of the two class means, not their gap. \qed

\paragraph{Part (ii): from margin to generalization.}
Given a 1-Lipschitz separator $f$ with population margin $\gamma_{a,b}$ as in (i), apply the standard Lipschitz-margin Rademacher bound~\cite{bartlett2002rademacher,koltchinskii2002empirical}: for a sample of size $n$, with probability $\ge 1-\delta$,
\[
\mathrm{err}(f) \;\le\; \widehat{\mathrm{err}}_{\gamma}(f) + \frac{2\,\mathfrak{R}_n(\mathcal{F}_{1\text{-Lip}})}{\gamma_{a,b}} + \sqrt{\frac{\log(1/\delta)}{2n}}.
\]
For 1-Lipschitz functions on $\mathbb{S}^{255}$, the Rademacher complexity satisfies $\mathfrak{R}_n(\mathcal{F}_{1\text{-Lip}}) = O(1/\sqrt{n})$~\cite{bartlett2002rademacher}. Since $\widehat{\mathrm{err}}_\gamma(f) = 0$ when the empirical margin matches the population margin (which holds with high probability by part (i)), the claim follows with $C_2$ absorbing the universal constants. \qed

\subsection{Extended Analysis}

\paragraph{Per-Benchmark Details.} DeepSafe shows particularly strong performance on benchmarks requiring intent discrimination (XSTest, 100PoisonMpts, DoNotAnswer), where generative guard models often exhibit high false positive rates. On larger, diverse benchmarks (WildGuardMix, AegisAI-v2), DeepSafe remains competitive with much larger generative models. The hyperbolic projection's hierarchical structure naturally separates benign discussion of sensitive topics from genuinely harmful content.

\paragraph{Inference Efficiency Details.} DeepSafe offers significant practical advantages: (1) no GPU required for inference after one-time embedding computation; (2) batched throughput of 50+ samples/s on CPU vs.\ 10--100 samples/s for generative models each requiring a full LLM forward pass; (3) deterministic outputs (identical scores for identical embeddings); and (4) privacy-preserving deployment without external API calls.

\subsection{Training--Evaluation Overlap Audit}
\label{app:overlap}

To verify the claim of strict data disjointness (\S\ref{sec:exp}, \S\ref{sec:discussion}), we conducted an 8-gram overlap audit between the full 389K training corpus and each of the 9 evaluation benchmarks. For every training sample and every test sample across all benchmarks, we computed the Jaccard similarity of their 8-gram sets using MinHash with 128 permutations. Any (train, test) pair exceeding 0.3 Jaccard similarity was flagged as a potential near-duplicate. Across all 9 benchmarks and the entire training corpus, zero pairs exceeded this threshold. Exact 8-gram overlap counts (stricter metric) similarly confirmed zero meaningful overlap. This audit provides hard evidence that DeepSafe's reported gains reflect generalization to unseen distributions, not in-distribution memorization.
