\begin{abstract}
Most production-grade safety guards are billion-parameter LLMs fine-tuned to emit binary safe/unsafe verdicts---accurate, but expensive at inference, GPU-bound, and opaque at the decision level. We ask a narrower question: can a small classifier on top of a frozen encoder match this accuracy if the representation space is geometrically structured for the task? We reformulate safety classification geometrically. A 7.3M-parameter projection head reorganizes frozen 4096-d Qwen3-Embedding-8B vectors(or 1024-d Qwen3-Embedding-0.6B vectors) into a 256-d intent-separable subspace using hyperbolic layers on the Poincar\'{e} ball---whose exponential volume mirrors a safe $\to$ pseudo-harmful $\to$ malicious gradient---regularized by prototype-anchored Sinkhorn optimal transport. A 0.1M-parameter residual classifier is then trained on the frozen subspace with mixup and temperature scaling. Given pre-computed embeddings, the full pipeline runs on a single CPU core at $>$50 samples/s. We compare our model pairwise against 9 production-grade guards spanning the WildGuard, Llama-Guard-3, ShieldGemma, Qwen3Guard, Granite-Guardian, and Beaver-Dam families on 9 widely-used safety benchmarks. Our model wins 80 of 81 (98.8\%) pairs in accuracy, with a mean lead of +12.3 absolute points overall and +14.0 points on the intent-sensitive subset. Furthermore, experiments demonstrate that hyperbolic geometry alone is inert, under standard BCE loss it behaves almost identically to Euclidean projection,its representational power is only activated by the full DeepSafe training objective.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
%========================================================================

Content safety has become a critical concern as large language models (LLMs) are deployed at scale in user-facing applications. The current landscape of post-hoc safety moderation encompasses two broad paradigms. \textbf{Encoder-based discriminative classifiers}---including OpenAI Moderation API, Perspective API, Detoxify, and HateBERT---use lightweight transformer encoders to score content, offering low latency and interpretable token-level signals but limited contextual reasoning. \textbf{Generative guard models}---such as WildGuard~\cite{vidgen2024introducing}, Llama~Guard~3~\cite{inan2023llama}, ShieldGemma, Qwen3Guard-Gen, and Granite~Guardian---fine-tune 1--9B parameter LLMs to output binary safety verdicts, achieving strong accuracy through generative reasoning at the cost of billion-parameter inference per query. Both paradigms share a common blind spot: they are optimized to discriminate safe from unsafe content, but neither is designed to reveal \emph{why} a particular input is classified as harmful---their decision boundaries remain opaque functions of token-level statistics.

A third, emerging line of work studies safety through the lens of \textbf{representation geometry}. Zou et al.~\cite{zou2023representation} showed that harmfulness concepts are linearly separable in LLM hidden states and can be manipulated via representation engineering. Arditi et al.~\cite{arditi2024refusal} identified a single refusal-mediating direction. Lee et al. probed harmful intent in embedding spaces. These findings collectively suggest that safety-relevant structure exists in the representation space and can be made explicit. However, prior representation-based safety classifiers have not closed the accuracy gap with large generative guards, and their geometric design choices---Euclidean linear probing, single-direction interventions---have not been systematically benchmarked against the hierarchical nature of safety taxonomies.

We argue that the core obstacle is \emph{representation collapse}: frozen embedding spaces such as Qwen3-Embedding~\cite{qwen3} lack the structural priors needed to separate malicious intent from benign discussion of the same topic. As shown in Figure~\ref{fig:tsne} (left), the original 4,096-dimensional space exhibits near-zero silhouette score ($\approx 0.01$), with safe, pseudo-harmful, and malicious content completely overlapping. The practical consequence is \textbf{intent blindness}---classifiers trained atop these embeddings routinely conflate ``How do I build a bomb to hurt people?'' with ``What are the dangers of building bombs?''---producing over-censorship that is measurable on benchmarks such as XSTest, OR-Bench, and PHTest.

To address this, we propose a two-stage framework: a geometrically principled projection head learns an intent-aware representation space, then a neural classifier is trained on the frozen projected representations. Four design choices advance beyond prior representation-based classifiers: (1)~\textbf{hyperbolic projection} on the Poincar\'{e} ball~\cite{nickel2017poincare,ganea2018hyperbolic}, whose exponential volume growth matches the safe $\to$ pseudo-harmful $\to$ malicious hierarchy; (2)~\textbf{learnable prototypes}~\cite{snell2017proto} with Sinkhorn optimal transport~\cite{cuturi2013sinkhorn} for distribution-level class separation with hierarchically-structured margins; (3)~a \textbf{learnable gate} $\beta$ that blends the hyperbolic pathway with a Euclidean residual pathway via skip connection; and (4)~a \textbf{residual neural classifier} (0.1M parameters) with mixup~\cite{zhang2018mixup} and temperature scaling~\cite{guo2017calibration} replacing the standard logistic regression probe. The total trainable footprint is 7.4M parameters; given pre-computed embeddings, the pipeline runs at $>$50 samples/s on CPU.

Our contributions are: (i)~we formalize \emph{intent blindness} as a measurable consequence of representation collapse and show hyperbolic geometry directly addresses it; (ii)~we demonstrate that a residual neural classifier on a hyperbolic-structured frozen subspace closes the accuracy gap to billion-parameter generative guard models; and (iii)~we provide the central finding that hyperbolic geometry and the structured loss interact synergistically---neither is sufficient alone ($|\Delta| \leq 0.003$ under standard BCE loss), yet together they yield +11.9 validation points.

\section{Related Work}
%========================================================================

\subsection{Post-Hoc Safety Classification}

Post-hoc safety moderation encompasses two established paradigms: \textbf{encoder-based discriminative classifiers} (OpenAI Moderation API, Perspective API, Detoxify, HateBERT) offering low latency but limited reasoning, and \textbf{generative guard models} (WildGuard~\cite{vidgen2024introducing}, Llama~Guard~3~\cite{inan2023llama}, ShieldGemma, Qwen3Guard-Gen, Granite~Guardian, Beaver-Dam) that fine-tune 1--9B LLMs for binary safety verdicts at billion-parameter inference cost~\cite{markov2023holistic}. Pre-deployment alignment (RLHF~\cite{ouyang2022training}, DPO~\cite{rafailov2024direct}, constitutional AI~\cite{bai2022constitutional}) aims for safer generation but provides no explicit post-hoc judgments. Our method operates on a geometrically reorganized representation space, combining encoder-level latency with generative-guard accuracy.

\subsection{Representation Engineering for Safety}

Recent work studies safety through representation geometry. Zou et al.~\cite{zou2023representation} demonstrated linear separability of harmfulness in LLM hidden states. Arditi et al.~\cite{arditi2024refusal} identified a single refusal-mediating direction. These findings establish that safety structure exists in representation space and can be made explicit. However, existing approaches operate on raw embedding spaces with linear probes, inheriting the representation collapse shown in Figure~\ref{fig:tsne}, and have not been benchmarked against hierarchical safety taxonomies. Our work reorganizes the space through hyperbolic projection and trains a nonlinear classifier on the structured subspace.

\subsection{Contrastive Learning and Counterfactuals}

Supervised contrastive learning~\cite{khosla2020supervised} and its NLP adaptations~\cite{gao2021simcse} learn structured representations from labeled data. Counterfactual augmentation improves robustness~\cite{kaushik2020learning}, and counterfactual pairs have been proposed as evaluation benchmarks~\cite{vidgen2024introducing}. Our hierarchical contrastive loss operates on multiple label granularities, and we incorporate counterfactual pairs directly into training via a repulsion loss targeting intent-level separation.

\section{Method}
%========================================================================

\subsection{Problem Formulation}

Let $\mathcal{D} = \{(\mathbf{x}_i, y_i^b, y_i^I)\}_{i=1}^N$ be a dataset where $y_i^b \in \{0, 1\}$ is a binary safety label and $y_i^I \in \{0, 1, 2\}$ follows our hierarchical taxonomy: Safe (0), Pseudo-Harmful (1: sensitive topics with legitimate purpose), and Malicious (2: explicitly harmful). Safe and Pseudo-Harmful map to $y^b = 0$; Malicious maps to $y^b = 1$. Let $f_\theta(\mathbf{x}) = \mathbf{e} \in \mathbb{R}^{4096}$ be a frozen Qwen3-Embedding-8B~\cite{qwen3} encoder. We learn a projection head $g_\phi: \mathbb{R}^{4096} \to \mathbb{R}^{256}$ and a neural classifier $h_\psi: \mathbb{R}^{256} \to [0,1]$.

\subsection{Projection Head Architecture}

The projection head uses dual hyperbolic layers with a learnable Euclidean-hyperbolic gate:

\paragraph{Dual Hyperbolic Neural Projection.} Safety labels form a natural hierarchy (Safe $\to$ Pseudo-Harmful $\to$ Malicious). Euclidean geometry poorly captures such hierarchical structure, as volume grows polynomially with radius while the number of hierarchical descendants grows exponentially~\cite{nickel2017poincare}. We therefore project embeddings into the Poincar\'{e} ball model of hyperbolic geometry with curvature $c=1.0$, where volume grows exponentially and naturally encodes tree-like hierarchies.

Given a frozen embedding $\mathbf{e} \in \mathbb{R}^{4096}$, we first L2-normalize to $\tilde{\mathbf{e}} = \mathbf{e} / \|\mathbf{e}\|_2$, then project to a tangent space $\mathbf{t} = \text{LayerNorm}(\mathbf{W}_{\text{in}} \tilde{\mathbf{e}})$. The exponential map $\exp_{\mathbf{0}}^c(\mathbf{t})$ lifts $\mathbf{t}$ onto the Poincar\'{e} ball:

\begin{align}
    \exp_{\mathbf{0}}^c(\mathbf{t}) = \tanh(\sqrt{c} \, \|\mathbf{t}\|) \frac{\mathbf{t}}{\sqrt{c} \, \|\mathbf{t}\|}
\end{align}

Within the hyperbolic space, we apply \textbf{two stacked} hyperbolic linear layers with GELU activations, following the hyperbolic neural network framework of~\cite{ganea2018hyperbolic}. Each hyperbolic linear layer applies M\"{o}bius matrix-vector multiplication $\mathbf{W} \otimes_c \mathbf{x} = \exp_{\mathbf{0}}^c(\mathbf{W} \log_{\mathbf{0}}^c(\mathbf{x}))$, followed by hyperbolic activation. After the hyperbolic layers, we apply the logarithmic map to return to Euclidean space:

\begin{align}
    \log_{\mathbf{0}}^c(\mathbf{x}) = \frac{\operatorname{arctanh}(\sqrt{c} \, \|\mathbf{x}\|)}{\sqrt{c} \, \|\mathbf{x}\|} \mathbf{x}
\end{align}

The hyperbolic-to-Euclidean transition layer concatenates the log-mapped features with their distance-to-origin in the Poincar\'{e} ball, which encodes a learned ``severity'' metric.

\paragraph{Learnable Gate Mechanism.} The hyperbolic pathway captures hierarchical safety structure, while a parallel Euclidean residual pathway preserves fine-grained semantic information. A learnable scalar gate $\beta = \sigma(w_\beta)$ adaptively blends the two:

\begin{align}
    \mathbf{h}_{\text{combined}} = \beta \cdot \mathbf{h}_{\text{hyp}} + (1-\beta) \cdot \mathbf{h}_{\text{res}} + \mathbf{W}_{\text{skip}} \tilde{\mathbf{e}}
\end{align}

The skip connection $\mathbf{W}_{\text{skip}} \tilde{\mathbf{e}}$ preserves input-level information and prevents vanishing signals through the deep projection.

The combined features are processed through LayerNorm, GELU activation, dropout (0.1), and a final linear projection to 256 dimensions, with batch normalization and L2 normalization producing the final output $\mathbf{z} \in \mathbb{S}^{255}$.

\subsection{Neural Classifier}

Unlike prior embedding-based safety classifiers that train a simple logistic regression probe, DeepSafe trains a \textbf{residual neural classifier} $h_\psi$ on the frozen projected representations. The classifier architecture is:

\begin{align}
    \mathbf{h}_1 &= \text{GELU}(\text{LayerNorm}(\mathbf{W}_1 \mathbf{z} + \mathbf{b}_1)) \quad (256 \to 256) \\
    \mathbf{h}_2 &= \mathbf{h}_1 + \text{GELU}(\text{LayerNorm}(\mathbf{W}_2 \mathbf{h}_1 + \mathbf{b}_2)) \quad (256 \to 128, \text{ residual}) \\
    \mathbf{h}_3 &= \text{GELU}(\text{LayerNorm}(\mathbf{W}_3 \mathbf{h}_2 + \mathbf{b}_3)) \quad (128 \to 64) \\
    \text{logits} &= \mathbf{W}_4 \mathbf{h}_3 + \mathbf{b}_4 \quad (64 \to 2)
\end{align}

Training uses AdamW with cosine annealing, class-weighted loss to handle label imbalance, mixup augmentation~\cite{zhang2018mixup} ($\alpha=0.2$) on projected embeddings, and temperature scaling~\cite{guo2017calibration} ($T=1.5$) for calibrated probabilities. The classifier adds only $\sim$0.1M parameters and is trained for up to 50 epochs with early stopping (patience 10).

\paragraph{Spectral Decorrelation.} We apply a Barlow Twins-style~\cite{zbontar2021barlow} regularization that penalizes off-diagonal elements of the feature correlation matrix, promoting diverse, non-redundant feature dimensions:

\begin{align}
    \mathcal{L}_{\text{decorr}} = \lambda_d \sum_{i \neq j} \left(\frac{1}{B} \mathbf{Z}^\top \mathbf{Z}\right)_{ij}^2
\end{align}

\subsection{Optimal Transport Class Regularization}

Standard contrastive loss operates at the instance level, which can be noisy and sensitive to outliers. We introduce a complementary distribution-level objective based on entropic optimal transport. For each class pair $(a, b)$, the Sinkhorn divergence~\cite{cuturi2013sinkhorn} provides a principled distance between the empirical distributions of their projected representations:

\begin{align}
    \mathcal{L}_{\text{OT}} = \sum_{c} S_\epsilon(\mathbf{Z}_c, \mathbf{Z}_c) - \lambda_{\text{inter}} \sum_{a < b} S_\epsilon(\mathbf{Z}_a, \mathbf{Z}_b)
\end{align}

where $S_\epsilon$ is the Sinkhorn divergence computed via the Sinkhorn-Knopp algorithm with entropic regularization $\epsilon$. The first term promotes intra-class compactness, while the second term encourages inter-class separation at the distribution level.

\subsection{Hierarchical Contrastive Loss}

We retain the hierarchical contrastive loss~\cite{khosla2020supervised} operating at two granularities:

\begin{align}
    \mathcal{L}_{\text{hier}} = \mathcal{L}_{\text{bin}} + \alpha \cdot \mathcal{L}_{\text{intent}}
\end{align}

where $\mathcal{L}_{\text{bin}}$ contrasts safe vs.\ unsafe samples, and $\mathcal{L}_{\text{intent}}$ contrasts the three fine-grained intent classes (Safe, Pseudo-Harmful, Malicious).

\subsection{Learnable Prototype Anchors}

To provide explicit structural guidance, we maintain learnable prototype vectors $\{\mathbf{p}_k\}_{k=0}^{2}$ in the projected space~\cite{snell2017proto}, one per intent class. The prototype loss applies hierarchical margins reflecting the intent taxonomy:

\begin{align}
    \mathcal{L}_{\text{proto}} = -\underbrace{\frac{1}{B}\sum_i \cos(\mathbf{z}_i, \mathbf{p}_{y_i})}_{\text{attraction}} + \lambda_p \underbrace{\frac{1}{B}\sum_i \sum_{c \neq y_i} \max(0, \cos(\mathbf{z}_i, \mathbf{p}_c) - m_{y_i,c})}_{\text{hierarchical repulsion}}
\end{align}

where the margin matrix $m_{y,c}$ encodes the semantic distance between intent classes: Safe$\leftrightarrow$Malicious uses the largest margin (0.5), Safe$\leftrightarrow$Pseudo-Harmful uses a smaller margin (0.25), and Pseudo-Harmful$\leftrightarrow$Malicious uses an intermediate margin (0.35).

\subsection{Full Training Objective}

\begin{align}
    \mathcal{L}_{\text{DeepSafe}} = \underbrace{\mathcal{L}_{\text{bin}} + \alpha \, \mathcal{L}_{\text{intent}}}_{\mathcal{L}_{\text{hier}}} + \lambda_{\text{ot}} \Big(\underbrace{\sum_{c} S_\epsilon(\mathbf{Z}_c, \mathbf{Z}_c) - \lambda_{\text{inter}} \sum_{a < b} S_\epsilon(\mathbf{Z}_a, \mathbf{Z}_b)}_{\mathcal{L}_{\text{OT}}}\Big) + \lambda_p \mathcal{L}_{\text{proto}} + \lambda_d \mathcal{L}_{\text{decorr}}
\end{align}

We use $\lambda_{\text{ot}}=0.3$, $\lambda_{\text{inter}}=0.5$, $\lambda_p=0.2$, $\alpha=2.0$, and $\lambda_d=0.005$. Training uses AdamW with cosine annealing (1e-3 to 1e-6) for up to 100 epochs with early stopping (patience 15).

\section{Experiments}

\subsection{Experimental Setup}

\paragraph{Dataset.} Our training set draws \textbf{exclusively} from WildGuardMix/train (50K samples), AegisAI Content Safety v2/train (20K samples), with optional auxiliary data from 100PoisonMpts/train and XGuard-Train-Open-200K. After augmentation, this yields approximately 389K training samples. Each sample is embedded using Qwen3-Embedding-8B (4096-dim)~\cite{qwen3}. Intent labels (Safe, Pseudo-Harmful, Malicious) are assigned using a keyword-based heuristic initially, with plans for LLM-based relabeling in future work. \textbf{All 9 evaluation benchmarks}---AegisAI-1.0, AegisAI-2.0/test, BeaverTails, CRiskEval, DoNotAnswer, MM-SafetyBench, SafetyBench, ToxicChat, and XSTest---\textbf{are used as 100\% held-out test sets}, with absolutely zero overlap between training and evaluation data.

\paragraph{Evaluation Benchmarks.} We evaluate on 9 fully held-out benchmark datasets: WildGuardMix/test (26,028 samples), AegisAI-v2 (10,019), AegisAI-v1 (3,237), ToxicChat (3,049), BeaverTails (3,000), XGuard (3,000), DoNotAnswer (281), 100PoisonMpts (272), and XSTest (135). These span diverse domains including general safety, toxicity, refusal detection, and poisoning attacks, and are completely disjoint from training data.

\paragraph{Baselines.} We compare DeepSafe against 9 state-of-the-art generative guard models:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
    \item \textbf{WildGuard} (7B): Mixed safety-harmfulness classifier from Allen AI.
    \item \textbf{Qwen3Guard-Gen-4B / 8B}: Generative safety classifiers from Alibaba.
    \item \textbf{Llama Guard 3 1B / 8B}: Meta's safety guard models.
    \item \textbf{ShieldGemma 2B / 9B}: Google's safety classifiers.
    \item \textbf{Granite Guardian 3.1-8B}: IBM's safety guard model.
    \item \textbf{Beaver-Dam-7B}: Safe RLHF-based safety classifier.
\end{itemize}

All SOTA baselines are re-evaluated on the exact same held-out data splits for fair comparison.

\paragraph{DeepSafe Configuration.} The projection head uses input dimension 4096, hidden dimension 768, output dimension 256, hyperbolic dimension 128, curvature $c=1.0$, dropout 0.1 (7.3M parameters). The neural classifier uses hidden dimensions [256, 128, 64] with residual connections, dropout 0.2, and temperature scaling $T=1.5$ ($\sim$0.1M parameters). Phase 1 (projection head) trains for up to 100 epochs with AdamW ($lr=5\times10^{-4}$, weight decay $10^{-4}$), cosine annealing to $10^{-6}$, batch size 256, early stopping patience 20. Phase 2 (neural classifier) trains for up to 50 epochs with AdamW ($lr=10^{-3}$, weight decay $10^{-5}$), mixup $\alpha=0.2$, batch size 512, early stopping patience 10. Loss weights: $\alpha=2.0$, $\lambda_{\text{ot}}=0.3$, $\lambda_{\text{inter}}=0.5$, $\lambda_{\text{proto}}=0.2$, $\lambda_{\text{decorr}}=0.005$, $\gamma=0.5$.

\paragraph{Metrics.} We report accuracy, macro-averaged F1, and ROC AUC for binary safety classification. For per-benchmark analysis, we report per-model accuracy and head-to-head win counts. Inference time measurements include embedding computation (DeepSafe) or model forward pass (SOTA) on an NVIDIA RTX 5090 GPU.

However, DeepSafe's ROC AUC of 0.9951 on this benchmark is the highest among all models, indicating superior ranking quality. On ToxicChat, where 92.8\% of samples are safe, DeepSafe's calibrated threshold ($t=0.87$) yields 0.9736 accuracy, surpassing ShieldGemma-9B (0.9635) with a higher AUC of 0.9675.

\subsection{Representation Quality}

DeepSafe's projection head learns a fundamentally reorganized representation space. The gap between original embedding space and projected space is substantial: the silhouette score increases from near-random clustering (silhouette $\approx 0.01$) to moderate separation ($\approx 0.35$ in 2D t-SNE space, $\approx 0.30$ in the original 256-dimensional projected space). The hyperbolic pathway's distance-to-origin metric naturally encodes safety severity, with malicious content mapped closer to the Poincar\'{e} ball boundary and safe content clustered near the origin.

\subsection{Per-Benchmark Analysis}

DeepSafe shows particularly strong performance on benchmarks requiring intent discrimination (XSTest, 100PoisonMpts, DoNotAnswer), where generative guard models often exhibit high false positive rates. The hyperbolic projection's hierarchical structure naturally separates benign discussion from genuinely harmful content, while per-benchmark threshold calibration corrects for class imbalance.

\subsection{Inference Efficiency}

DeepSafe runs on CPU after embedding pre-computation: 7.4M total trainable parameters vs.\ 4B--9B for generative guards, 50+ samples/s throughput, deterministic outputs, and privacy-preserving local deployment (Figure~\ref{fig:speed_vs_accuracy}).

\section{Discussion}
%========================================================================

The strong performance of DeepSafe derives from the synergistic combination of hyperbolic geometry, distribution-level optimal transport supervision, learnable prototypes, and the residual neural classifier. \Cref{tab:ablation} confirms that no single component accounts for the full gain; rather, the hyperbolic projection establishes a representation space that the structured loss exploits. Figure~\ref{fig:ablation} provides the critical interaction evidence: under standard BCE loss, hyperbolic and Euclidean projections are indistinguishable ($|\Delta| \leq 0.003$); the +11.9-point gain emerges only from their synergy.

\subsection{Limitations and Future Work}

\paragraph{Embedding Dependency.} DeepSafe's performance is bounded by the quality of the frozen Qwen3-Embedding-8B encoder. Fine-tuning the encoder jointly with the projection head, or using an encoder specifically pre-trained for safety-critical content, could yield further gains.

\paragraph{Hyperbolic Curvature.} The Poincar\'{e} ball curvature $c=1.0$ is treated as a fixed hyperparameter. Learning the curvature adaptively---or using mixed-curvature products of multiple manifolds~\cite{gu2018mixed}---could further improve hierarchical representation quality.

\paragraph{Counterfactual Data.} The counterfactual pair repulsion loss is limited by the small number of synthetic pairs (432 pairs). Scaling up pair generation using LLM-based paraphrasing could substantially improve intent-level separation, particularly for Chinese and other non-English languages.

\paragraph{Evaluation Scope.} Our 9 benchmarks cover English and (partially) Chinese safety domains. Evaluation on broader multilingual safety datasets and more diverse harm categories (e.g., multimodal safety, code safety) remains future work.

%========================================================================
\section{Conclusion}
%========================================================================

We introduced DeepSafe, a two-stage safety classification framework that combines a geometrically principled hyperbolic projection head with a neural classifier, achieving embedding-based safety classification that matches or exceeds large generative guard models on a majority of benchmarks for the first time. Our key innovations include: (1) dual hyperbolic neural layers that deeply encode the hierarchical safety taxonomy, (2) a learnable gate mechanism that adaptively blends hyperbolic and Euclidean representations with skip connections, (3) optimal transport regularization for distribution-level class separation, (4) learnable prototype anchors with hierarchical margins, (5) a residual neural classifier with mixup augmentation operating on the frozen projected space, and (6) per-benchmark decision threshold calibration that corrects for class imbalance without retraining.

Our experiments across 9 held-out benchmark datasets demonstrate that DeepSafe with calibrated thresholds achieves the best accuracy on 8 of 9 benchmarks, while using orders of magnitude fewer parameters and operating efficiently on CPU. On the sole remaining benchmark (WildGuardMix), DeepSafe achieves the highest AUC (0.9951) among all models. The per-benchmark calibration proves essential: thresholds learned from training data correct for varying class distributions (e.g., $t=0.08$ for cybersecurity content vs.\ $t=0.95$ for predominantly safe datasets), yielding accuracy gains of up to +28.47\% on heavily imbalanced benchmarks. The framework advances the state of the art in embedding-based safety classification, demonstrating that lightweight frozen-embedding approaches can match or exceed resource-intensive generative guard models.

We believe this work establishes a new paradigm for safety classification: rather than treating it as a purely generative task requiring large language models, DeepSafe shows that geometrically structured frozen embeddings, combined with a learned neural classifier and calibrated decision thresholds, can achieve competitive or superior results. This opens the door to fast, private, and cost-effective safety filtering for production LLM deployments.

\subsection{Extended Analysis}

\paragraph{Per-Benchmark Details.} DeepSafe shows particularly strong performance on benchmarks requiring intent discrimination (XSTest, 100PoisonMpts, DoNotAnswer), where generative guard models often exhibit high false positive rates. On larger, diverse benchmarks (WildGuardMix, AegisAI-v2), DeepSafe remains competitive with much larger generative models. The hyperbolic projection's hierarchical structure naturally separates benign discussion of sensitive topics from genuinely harmful content.

\paragraph{Inference Efficiency Details.} DeepSafe offers significant practical advantages: (1) no GPU required for inference after one-time embedding computation; (2) batched throughput of 50+ samples/s on CPU vs.\ 10--100 samples/s for generative models each requiring a full LLM forward pass; (3) deterministic outputs (identical scores for identical embeddings); and (4) privacy-preserving deployment without external API calls.
